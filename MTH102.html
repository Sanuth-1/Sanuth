Chapter 1: Functions, Limits, and Continuity

This foundational chapter establishes the essential building blocks of calculus by rigorously defining functions, exploring their graphical representations, and introducing the pivotal concepts of limits and continuity. A thorough understanding of these topics is indispensable for comprehending the behavior of functions and for the subsequent development of differentiation and integration.

1.1 Functions of a Real Variable

A function of a real variable describes a relationship where each input value (independent variable, x) corresponds to exactly one output value (dependent variable, y or f(x)). Examples include speed as a function of distance and time, or voltage as a function of current and resistance (Ohm's Law).

Function notation, f(x), represents the output for input x. Graphs visually represent function behavior, domain (possible inputs), and range (possible outputs), showing increasing/decreasing intervals, intercepts, and asymptotes. Combinations and inverse functions are also studied.

Example: Graph of y = x2

1.2 Limits

A limit describes the value a function approaches as its input approaches a certain point. One-sided limits (left-hand and right-hand) must exist and be equal for a general limit to exist.

The formal Epsilon-Delta definition: For every ε > 0, there exists a δ > 0 such that if 0 < |x - a| < δ, then |f(x) - L| < ε.

Evaluation techniques include direct substitution, factoring, conjugate method, and analyzing behavior for limits to infinity. Understanding limits is crucial for defining derivatives and integrals.

Limit Rules

Rule Name Formal Statement Illustrative Example
Sum Rule limx→a [f(x) + g(x)] = limx→a f(x) + limx→a g(x) limx→2 (x2 + 3x) = 4 + 6 = 10
The limit of a sum is the sum of the limits, provided individual limits exist.
Difference Rule limx→a [f(x) - g(x)] = limx→a f(x) - limx→a g(x) limx→1 (5x - x3) = 5 - 1 = 4
The limit of a difference is the difference of the limits, provided individual limits exist.
Product Rule limx→a [f(x) ⋅ g(x)] = limx→a f(x) ⋅ limx→a g(x) limx→3 (x ⋅ (x+1)) = 3 ⋅ 4 = 12
The limit of a product is the product of the limits, provided individual limits exist.
Constant Multiple Rule limx→a [c ⋅ f(x)] = c ⋅ limx→a f(x) limx→0 (4 sin x) = 4 ⋅ 0 = 0
A constant factor can be pulled out of the limit operation.
Quotient Rule limx→a [f(x)g(x)] = limx→a f(x)limx→a g(x), if limx→a g(x) ≠ 0 limx→2 (x2x+1) = 43
The limit of a quotient is the quotient of the limits, provided the limit of the denominator is not zero.

1.3 Continuity

A function f(x) is continuous at a point a if: 1. f(a) exists, 2. limx→a f(x) exists, and 3. limx→a f(x) = f(a). Continuous functions have no breaks, jumps, or holes.

Types of discontinuities:

  • Removable: A hole in the graph; limit exists but f(a) is undefined or different.
  • Jump: Left-hand and right-hand limits exist but are unequal.
  • Infinite: Function approaches ±∞; associated with vertical asymptotes.

Sums, differences, products, and quotients (non-zero denominator) of continuous functions are also continuous. Continuity is essential for differentiability.

Chapter 2: Differential Calculus: Theory and Applications

This chapter introduces the core concept of the derivative, explores various techniques for computing derivatives, and demonstrates their wide-ranging applications in analyzing function behavior and solving real-world problems.

2.1 The Derivative

The derivative, f'(x) or dydx, is defined as: limh→0 f(x+h) - f(x)h.

It represents the instantaneous rate of change and the slope of the tangent line to the curve at a point.

Physical interpretation: If s(t) is position, s'(t) = v(t) is velocity, and v'(t) = a(t) is acceleration (so a(t) = s''(t)).

Example: Function y = x2 (blue) and its tangent at x=1 (red).

2.2 Techniques of Differentiation

Mastering differentiation techniques is crucial. Key rules include:

Differentiation Rules

Rule Name Formal Statement Example
Constant Rule ddx(c) = 0 ddx(7) = 0
The derivative of a constant is zero.
Power Rule ddx(xn) = nxn-1 ddx(x5) = 5x4
Multiply by the exponent, then subtract one from the exponent.
Sum/Difference ddx[f(x) ± g(x)] = f'(x) ± g'(x) ddx(x2 + sin x) = 2x + cos x
The derivative of a sum/difference is the sum/difference of the derivatives.
Product Rule ddx[f(x)g(x)] = f(x)g'(x) + g(x)f'(x) ddx(x2sin x) = x2cos x + (sin x)(2x)
"First times derivative of second, plus second times derivative of first."
Quotient Rule ddx[f(x)g(x)] = g(x)f'(x) - f(x)g'(x)[g(x)]2 ddx(cos xx) = x(-sin x) - (cos x)(1)x2
"Low d-high minus high d-low, square the bottom and away we go."
Chain Rule ddx[f(g(x))] = f'(g(x))g'(x) ddx(sin(x2)) = cos(x2)(2x)
Derivative of the outer function (keeping inner function) times derivative of the inner function.

Implicit Differentiation: Used when y is not explicitly a function of x. Differentiate both sides w.r.t x, treating y as y(x) and using Chain Rule for terms with y.

Higher-Order Derivatives: Second derivative f''(x), third f'''(x), etc., provide info like concavity.

2.3 Applications of Differentiation

Maxima and Minima (Optimization): Find max/min values by finding critical points (f'(x)=0 or undefined) and using first/second derivative tests.

Curve Sketching: Analyze f'(x) for increasing/decreasing intervals and extrema. Analyze f''(x) for concavity and inflection points.

Related Rates: Find rate of change of one quantity by relating it to rates of other quantities using Chain Rule (differentiate w.r.t time).

L'Hôpital's Rule: For indeterminate limits (0/0 or ∞/∞), limx→a f(x)g(x) = limx→a f'(x)g'(x).

Physical Interpretations: Deeper understanding of velocity vs. speed, speeding up/slowing down based on signs of v(t) and a(t).

Chapter 3: Integral Calculus: Foundations and Applications

This chapter introduces integration as the inverse of differentiation, develops evaluation techniques, and explores applications like calculating areas and volumes.

3.1 Antiderivatives and Indefinite Integrals

An antiderivative F(x) of f(x) satisfies F'(x) = f(x). The indefinite integral ∫f(x) dx = F(x) + C, where C is the constant of integration, representing a family of functions.

Basic Indefinite Integration Rules

Rule Name Formal Statement Example
Constant Rule ∫ k dx = kx + C ∫ 5 dx = 5x + C
The integral of a constant k is kx plus the constant of integration C.
Power Rule (n ≠ -1) ∫ xn dx = xn+1n+1 + C ∫ x3 dx = x44 + C
Increase the power by 1, divide by the new power. Not applicable for n = -1.
Log Rule ∫ 1x dx = ln|x| + C ∫ 1x dx = ln|x| + C
The integral of 1/x is the natural logarithm of the absolute value of x.
Exponential Rule ∫ ex dx = ex + C ∫ ex dx = ex + C
The integral of ex is ex itself.

3.2 Techniques of Integration

Substitution (u-substitution): Reverse of Chain Rule. Choose u, find du, transform integral.

Integration by Parts: From product rule: ∫u dv = uv - ∫v du. Use LIATE (Log, Inverse Trig, Algebraic, Trig, Exponential) to choose u.

Partial Fractions: For rational functions. Decompose into simpler fractions based on denominator factors.

3.3 Definite Integrals

Defined as limit of Riemann sums (approximating area with rectangles). ∫ab f(x) dx.

Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (FTC):

  1. If F'(x) = f(x), then ∫ab f(x) dx = F(b) - F(a).
  2. ddx ∫ax f(t) dt = f(x).

Properties include order of limits, zero interval, constant multiple, sum/difference, interval splitting.

3.4 Applications of Integration

Areas: Area under curve ∫ab f(x) dx. Area between curves ∫ab [f(x) - g(x)] dx (upper - lower).

Example: Area under y = x2 from x=0 to x=2.

Volumes of Solids of Revolution:

  • Disk Method: π ∫ab [f(x)]2 dx
  • Washer Method: π ∫ab (R(x)2 - r(x)2) dx
  • Shell Method: 2π ∫ab x f(x) dx (for rotation about y-axis)

Reduction Formulae: Express In in terms of Ik (k < n).

Approximate Integration: Trapezium Rule, Simpson's Rule for numerical estimates.

Chapter 4: Foundations of Numbers and Basic Algebra

This chapter covers fundamental algebraic concepts and number systems, prerequisites for advanced topics.

4.1 Elementary Set Theory

Set: collection of distinct objects (elements). Universal set (U), empty set (∅). Finite/infinite sets.

Operations: Union (∪), Intersection (∩), Complement (Ac). Disjoint sets: no common elements.

Venn diagrams visually represent set relationships. Useful for counting and logic problems.

4.2 Real Numbers

Real numbers (ℝ) include all numbers on the number line (rational + irrational).

Classification: Natural (ℕ), Whole (𝕎), Integers (ℤ), Rational (ℚ - fractions p/q), Irrational (𝔽 - non-repeating/non-terminating decimals like √2, π, e).

Properties: Commutative, Associative, Distributive, Identity.

4.3 Quadratic Equations

Standard form: ax2 + bx + c = 0 (a ≠ 0). Max two roots (real/complex).

Solution Methods: Factoring, Square Roots, Completing the Square, Quadratic Formula: x = -b ± √(b2 - 4ac)2a, Graphing.

Discriminant D = b2 - 4ac: If D > 0 (2 distinct real roots), D = 0 (1 real repeated root), D < 0 (2 complex roots).

Sum of roots (α + β) = -b/a. Product of roots (αβ) = c/a.

4.4 Binomial Theorem

Expands (a + b)n: ∑k=0n (n!k!(n-k)!) an-k bk. The term (n!k!(n-k)!) is nCk or (nk).

Applications: Combinatorics, probability (binomial distribution), polynomial approximation, financial math, series expansions (Taylor/Maclaurin).

4.5 Systems of Linear Equations

Two or more linear equations with same variables. Solution satisfies all equations.

Methods: Graphical (intersection of lines), Substitution (solve for one variable, substitute into other), Elimination (add/subtract equations to eliminate a variable).

Chapter 5: Complex Numbers and Their Geometry

This chapter introduces complex numbers, their operations, geometric representation, and De Moivre's Theorem.

5.1 Complex Numbers: Algebraic Operations

Imaginary unit i = √-1. Complex number z = a + bi (a=real part, b=imaginary part).

Addition: (a + bi) + (c + di) = (a+c) + (b+d)i

Subtraction: (a + bi) - (c + di) = (a-c) + (b-d)i

Multiplication: (a + bi)(c + di) = (ac - bd) + (ad + bc)i (since i2 = -1)

Division: Multiply numerator and denominator by conjugate of denominator (c - di for c + di).

5.2 The Argand Diagram

Geometric representation on a plane: horizontal real axis, vertical imaginary axis. z = a + bi is point (a,b).

Modulus |z| = r = √(a2 + b2) (distance from origin).

Argument arg(z) = θ (angle from positive real axis). Careful with quadrant for tan-1(b/a).

Polar form: z = r(cosθ + i sinθ).

Argand Diagram: Point z = 3 + 4i

5.3 De Moivre's Theorem and Nth Roots of Unity

De Moivre's Theorem: For z = r(cosθ + i sinθ), zn = rn(cos(nθ) + i sin(nθ)).

Nth roots of unity: Solutions to zn = 1. There are n distinct roots. Express 1 = 1(cos(2πk) + i sin(2πk)). Roots are zk = cos(2πkn) + i sin(2πkn) for k = 0, 1, ..., n-1.

Properties: Modulus 1 (on unit circle), evenly spaced arguments, form vertices of a regular n-gon. Sum of roots is 0 for n ≥ 2.

Chapter 7: Sequences, Series, and Mathematical Induction

Introduces sequences, series, convergence/divergence, and mathematical induction.

7.1 Mathematical Induction

Proof technique for statements P(n) for integers n ≥ 1.

  1. Basis Step: Verify P(1) is true.
  2. Inductive Hypothesis: Assume P(k) is true for some integer k ≥ 1.
  3. Inductive Step: Show if P(k) is true, then P(k+1) is true.

Example: Prove 1+2+...+n = n(n+1)2.

7.2 Real Sequences and Series

Sequence: ordered list of numbers. Series: sum of terms in a sequence.

Convergence: Series sum approaches a finite number. Divergence: Sum is infinite or oscillates.

Basic Convergence Tests: (Refer to Table 7.1 in source for Divergence Test, Geometric Series Test, P-Series Test).

Geometric Series ∑arn converges if |r| < 1 to a1-r.

7.3 Arithmetic Progressions (AP)

Common difference d. Nth term un = a + (n-1)d.

Sum of N terms SN = N2(2a + (N-1)d) = N2(a + l).

7.4 Geometric Progressions (GP)

Common ratio r. Nth term an = arn-1.

Sum of N terms SN = a(1-rN)1-r (for r ≠ 1).

Infinite GP sum S∞ = a1-r if |r| < 1.

8.3 Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors
